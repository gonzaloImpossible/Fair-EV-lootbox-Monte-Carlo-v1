2. The model in one paragraph

11 partner collections lend $74,288 of NFTs as inventory. Rarible adds $25,000 cash as a working-capital buffer. Users buy $960 boxes, each resolving to one of four outcomes (headline / high / mid / low NFT, or "1 shard"). After each NFT pull, the gacha offers to buy the NFT back instantly at 94% of its floor price — most users accept (~63% blended acceptance). When inventory drops below 50% (or the headline tier empties), the operator buys missing NFTs back from the open market, paid from the cashbox. At end of the weekly campaign, debt is repaid to lenders first, then Rarible takes its capital back, then the remaining profit is split.

4b. The auto-buyback flywheel

  • User pulls → operator offers buyback at 94% of floor
  • If accepted: NFT returns to pool (free to be pulled again), operator pays the 94% out of cashbox, pockets the 6% spread
  • If kept: NFT permanently leaves the pool; lender is owed its value at settlement
  • ~63% blended acceptance means most pulls cycle through the pool multiple times during the week

4c. Restock

  • Triggered when total inventory < 50% (38 NFTs) OR when the headline tier is empty
  • Operator buys missing NFTs from the open market at floor, paid from cashbox
  • Reduces lender debt accordingly
  • Expected: ~8 restocks per 1,000-pull campaign

4d. End-of-campaign settlement (waterfall)

1. CASHBOX (at end of campaign)
       = Sum box revenue - Sum auto-buyback payouts
                         - Sum restock costs
                         - Sum consolation costs

2. SENIOR LENDER DEBT REPAID
       = min(cashbox, remaining lender debt)
       Lenders made whole on NFTs that left the pool
       and weren't restocked.

3. OPERATOR CAPITAL RETURNED
       = min(cashbox-post-debt, $25,000)
       Rarible gets its working-capital buffer back.

4. PROFIT = remaining cashbox
       +--------------------------------------------------------------+
       |  OPERATOR FEE             = 50% × profit     -> Rarible      |
       |  +-----------------------------------------------+           |
       |  | POST-FEE PROFIT  = 50% × profit              |           |
       |  |   pro-rata to capital:                       |           |
       |  |     25.18% -> Rarible (as lender)            |           |
       |  |     74.82% -> External NFT lenders           |           |
       |  +-----------------------------------------------+           |
       +--------------------------------------------------------------+

   Rarible total share   = 50% + 50% × 25.18% = 62.6% of profit
   External lender total = 50% × 74.82%        = 37.4% of profit

6. User experience

  • Max ROI per box: +961% (10.6×) if user pulls a Pudgy and keeps it.
  • Worst outcome: 1 shard (worth 1/5 of a future box = $192 perceived value). Critically, the "you got nothing" scenario doesn't exist — every pull delivers something the user can keep or sell.
  • Mid-tier "consolation prize" feel: ~52% of pulls produce a mid- or low-tier NFT, where the auto-buyback offer typically becomes the better choice (instant cash at 94% of floor).
  • Per-pull expected value: ~$865, on a $960 box → ~10% implied edge. Beezie / Courtyard run wider edges (12-15%).

Three operational regimes:
  • 🔴 Danger zone (≤ 100 pulls) — 8–22% chance of capital loss. Mean ROI is positive but p5 ROI is negative. Lender returns barely above par (102–109%). Restock flywheel does not engage.
  • 🟡 Marginal zone (100–200 pulls) — cap-loss drops 8% → 2%. Operator p5 ROI turns positive ~pull 150. Restocks start firing reliably. This is the launch-viability threshold.
  • 🟢 Scaling zone (≥ 200 pulls) — 0% cap-loss, ROI positive at every percentile, flywheel fully active. Linear scaling on revenue.

11. Floor-shock stress test

Each scenario applies a one-shot floor change to Pudgy Penguins at pull 500 and lets the campaign continue. The model re-prices outstanding lender debt at the new floor and pays future restocks at the new floor.

Three observations from the stress test
  1. Symmetric P&L on Rarible's side: +50% floor → −14% ROI; −50% floor → +14% ROI. Roughly linear in the floor move.
  2. Asymmetric pain on lenders: Pudgy team WINS on a floor up-move (their remaining NFTs appreciate); Pudgy team LOSES on a floor crash (consumed NFTs worth less in cash settlement, fewer surviving NFTs in pool). Other lenders move the opposite direction because the pro-rata profit pool is what shifts.
  3. Real tail risk only at extreme moves (+100%): 0.8% cap-loss probability appears. Mitigation: bump initial_cashbox from $25k → $35k to cover this tail.

12. Risks

  1. NFT floor volatility — A sudden +50% floor jump on a heavily-consumed collection inflates momentary debt and restock cost by ~50%. Mitigation: bigger cashbox buffer, or a per-campaign floor re-pricing trigger above a threshold move. (Quantified in §10.)
  2. Open-market liquidity for restock — ~$388k of NFT purchases per campaign. Concentrated on Pudgies, Lil Pudgys, Doodles, Rektguy. On thinner markets (Quirkies, Moonbirds) the operator's buying could move floors against itself. Mitigation: split restocks into multiple smaller buys, or use Reservoir aggregator for slippage management.
  3. Cold-start demand risk — Model assumes 1,000 box pulls of demand per week. Below 200 pulls the model becomes structurally risky (see §10): 22%/15%/8% cap-loss probability at 25/50/100 pulls respectively. Mitigation: require ≥200 paid pre-orders before unlocking inventory, OR shrink p_tier_headline from 1% → 0.5% to reduce the tail catastrophe.
  4. Pudgy floor dependence — Pudgy Penguins is 27% of pool value AND the sole headline tier. If Pudgy floor drops 50% mid-campaign, Pudgy team's recovery drops to 169%. Mitigation: side-letter agreement on settlement valuation, or partial-floor-coverage.
  5. Partnership concentration — All 11 partners must agree to lend on the same terms. If one drops late, tier mix changes and box price needs recomputation.
  6. Regulatory / lootbox classification — Not addressed here. Legal needs to confirm the auto-buyback mechanic doesn't reclassify the product under gambling regulations.

13. Recommendation

Approve $25,000 of Rarible treasury capital for a one-week pilot using the industry_calibrated configuration, conditional on a credible demand forecast of ≥200 paid box pulls for the week. Expected outcomes at full volume (1,000 pulls): Rarible profit $130k (520% ROI), 11 partners recover 2× their lent value, capital-loss probability 0%.
Do NOT approve if demand forecast is below 200 pulls/week. See §10: at 100 pulls the cap-loss probability is 7.8%; at 50 pulls it rises to 14.8%; at 25 pulls, 22.3%. The only structural mitigations are (a) pre-committed demand, or (b) shrinking the headline-tier probability to reduce the tail catastrophe. A bigger cashbox alone does not change the loss probability at these volumes — it just dilutes severity.

Pilot-week unlocks the data we need to verify the two most uncertain assumptions: real user acceptance rate (modeled at 63%) and effective shard redemption rate (modeled at 100%, likely 30–60% in practice).

14. Next steps

  1. Partnership commitments: confirm written intent from each of the 11 collections to lend the specified NFT counts under the waterfall terms outlined in §4d.
  2. Settlement infrastructure: design and deploy the multi-sig / on-chain settlement contract that automates the waterfall.
  3. UX prototype: build the pull → auto-buyback flow with default-to-instant-sell behaviour and friction-on-keep for low-tier pulls.
  4. Legal review: confirm regulatory classification per market.
  5. Stress tests: run additional floor-shock and demand-shortfall scenarios beyond what's reported (§9, §10).
  6. Pilot: launch a single-week campaign with full instrumentation, then re-calibrate model parameters from observed user behaviour.

Appendix A — Model assumptions

See simulation_assumptions.py for the full declarative list. Highlights:

  • Floor prices held constant within the campaign (except in stress tests, §10)
  • Demand deterministic at 1,000 pulls (no stochastic demand modeling yet, but §9 covers shortfall settlement)
  • User acceptance Bernoulli, independent across pulls
  • No gas fees / opex / CAC costs modeled
  • Inventory restock is instantaneous at floor (no slippage)
  • Single-campaign analysis only (no multi-week carryover)
