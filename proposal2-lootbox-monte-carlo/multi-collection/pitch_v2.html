Rarible Multi-Collection NFT Gacha v2 · pre-sale design

Internal Approval Memo · Updated 2026-05-28 · Monte Carlo simulation (1,000 paths × 1,000 pulls/campaign) · Inventory snapshot 2026-05-25 · Auto-buyback discount 8% (user gets 92% of floor) · v1 ($960 flat) archived in archive/pitch_v1_flat960.html

1. Executive summary

A capital-light, inventory-lent NFT gacha. Rarible plays two roles — operator and small co-lender — and walks away with ~562% ROI on $25k of working capital per weekly campaign in expectation. The v2 design adds a price-discriminated pre-sale (first 200 boxes at $900, remainder at $990) backed by a refund-guarantee gate that eliminates the cold-start tail risk identified in v1. Auto-buyback discount is 8% — users get 92% of NFT floor on instant-sell.

2. The model in one paragraph

11 partner collections lend $74,288 of NFTs as inventory. Rarible adds $25,000 cash as a working-capital buffer. A pre-sale phase sells the first 200 boxes at $900 each, refundable if the 200-box threshold isn't met; the rest of the campaign sells at $990. Each box resolves to one of four outcomes (headline / high / mid / low NFT, or "1 shard"). After each NFT pull, the gacha offers to buy the NFT back instantly at 92% of its floor price — most users accept (~62% blended acceptance). When inventory drops below 50% (or the headline tier empties), the operator buys missing NFTs back from the open market, paid from the cashbox. End of the weekly campaign: debt is repaid to lenders first, then Rarible takes its capital back, then the remaining profit is split (62.6% Rarible / 37.4% external lenders).

Reading the table: a single $990 box has a 0.5% chance of pulling each specific Pudgy Penguin (10.6× max return if kept), a 5.45% chance of an Azuki, a 16% chance of a Sappy Seal, etc. "User keeps" = 1 − P(accept auto-buyback). Floor pulls (Sappy / Normies) are dumped back 85% of the time; headline pulls (Pudgy) are kept 95% of the time. Within-tier sampling is uniform so individual-NFT probability scales with how many of that NFT the partner contributed.

4c. Auto-buyback flywheel

  • User pulls → operator offers buyback at 92% of floor
  • If accepted: NFT returns to pool, operator pays the 92% out of cashbox, pockets the 8% spread
  • If kept: NFT permanently leaves the pool; lender is owed its value at settlement
  • ~63% blended acceptance means most pulls cycle through the pool multiple times

4d. Restock

  • Triggered when total inventory < 50% (38 NFTs) OR when the headline tier is empty
  • Operator buys missing NFTs from open market at floor, paid from cashbox
  • Reduces lender debt accordingly
  • Expected: ~8 restocks per 1,000-pull campaign

5. Pre-sale design NEW IN v2

v2 introduces a two-tier price structure plus a refund-guarantee gate that eliminates the cold-start risk identified in v1.

5a. The problem being solved

v1 (flat $960 per box) showed a structural failure mode at low volumes:

  • 22% chance of operator capital loss if the campaign only sells 25 boxes
  • 15% chance at 50 boxes
  • 8% chance at 100 boxes

The cause is structural: at low volumes, the cashbox hasn't accumulated enough revenue to absorb a single early Pudgy hold ($10,191 in instant debt against ~$25k buffer + thin box revenue). The flywheel doesn't engage. Raising the buffer doesn't fix this (the cap-loss probability is volume-dependent, not capital-dependent) — we tested this in v1 and the matrix was flat across buffer sizes.

5b. The v2 design

5c. The refund-guarantee gate

If fewer than 200 paid pre-orders land by the deadline, every pre-sale buyer is refunded automatically and the campaign does not open. Operator cost on miss: $0. The buffer stays unspent and gets redeployed next cycle. The campaign only goes live when the demand floor is provably above the cold-start risk zone.

5d. Why this works (and why a price hike alone wouldn't)

Pre-sale by itself increases low-volume risk (less revenue per box means slower cashbox accumulation):

The refund guarantee is what converts this from a risk profile into a binary outcome:

  • Pre-sale hits 200 → campaign opens → expected 1,000-pull run → 0% cap-loss, 549% ROI
  • Pre-sale misses 200 → automatic refund → 0% cap-loss, 0% ROI, capital redeployed

Without the gate, v2 would be strictly worse than v1 at low volumes. With the gate, v2 is strictly better at full volume and equivalent (= cancelled) when conditions aren't met.

5e. Side benefits to users

  • Pre-sale boxes offer a $71 better max-ROI ($10,191 Pudgy / $900 = +1,032% vs $10,191 / $990 = +929%). Marketing angle: "best deal of the campaign goes to early supporters."
  • Refund-guarantee removes user-side risk — pre-sale users only pay if the campaign actually runs. Lowers psychological barrier to early commitment.

7. User experience

  • Max ROI per pre-sale box: +1,032% (11.3×) on a Pudgy hit. Pre-sale users get the campaign's best max payout.
  • Max ROI per regular box: +929% (10.3×) on a Pudgy hit. Still a major prize.
  • Worst outcome: 1 shard (worth 1/5 of a future regular box = $198 perceived value). Every pull delivers something the user can keep or sell.
  • Mid-tier "consolation prize" feel: ~52% of pulls produce a mid- or low-tier NFT, where the auto-buyback offer at 92% of floor typically becomes the better choice.
  • Per-pull expected value: ~$870; on the regular $990 box → ~12% implied edge. Beezie / Courtyard run wider edges (12-15%).

13. Risks

  1. NFT floor volatility — A sudden +50% floor jump on a heavily-consumed collection inflates momentary debt and restock cost by ~50%. Mitigation: bigger cashbox buffer, or per-campaign floor re-pricing trigger above a threshold move. (Quantified in §12.)
  2. Open-market liquidity for restock — ~$388k of NFT purchases per campaign. Concentrated on Pudgies, Lil Pudgys, Doodles, Rektguy. Mitigation: split restocks into multiple smaller buys, or use Reservoir aggregator for slippage management.
  3. Cold-start demand risk — Below 200 pulls the model becomes structurally risky (see §11): 27%/23%/15% cap-loss probability at 25/50/100 pulls. Mitigation in v2: the refund-guarantee gate ensures the campaign only opens above 200 paid commits. Without the gate, v2 is strictly worse than v1 at low volumes.
  4. Pre-sale gate operational risk — The refund mechanism must execute reliably. If pre-sale undersells AND Rarible runs the campaign anyway (override), cap-loss probability spikes. Mitigation: automated refund triggered by smart-contract or off-chain deadline; no manual override.
  5. Pudgy floor dependence — Pudgy Penguins is 27% of pool value AND the sole headline tier. If Pudgy floor crashes mid-campaign, Pudgy team's recovery drops. Mitigation: side-letter on settlement valuation.
  6. Partnership concentration — All 11 partners must agree on the same waterfall terms. If one drops late, tier mix changes and box pricing needs recomputation.
  7. Regulatory / lootbox classification — Not addressed here. Legal needs to confirm the auto-buyback mechanic + pre-sale + refund don't reclassify the product under gambling regulations.

14. Recommendation

Approve $25,000 of Rarible treasury capital for a one-week pilot using the v2 industry_calibrated configuration, conditional on the refund-guarantee gate being implemented and enforced. Expected outcomes at full volume (1,000 pulls): Rarible profit $140,588 (562% ROI), 11 partners recover 2.13× their lent value, capital-loss probability 0%.
The refund-guarantee gate is non-negotiable for v2. If the gate is dropped and the campaign runs at sub-200 volume, expected cap-loss probability is 6-27% with mean losses $5,500-$8,600 per affected path. The +$11k operator-take uplift over v1 only materializes when the full 1,000 pulls happen.

Pilot-week unlocks the data we need to verify the two most uncertain assumptions: real user acceptance rate (modeled at 63%) and effective shard redemption rate (modeled at 100%, likely 30–60% in practice).

15. Next steps

  1. Partnership commitments: confirm written intent from each of the 11 collections under the v2 waterfall terms outlined in §4d.
  2. Refund-guarantee infrastructure: design the on-chain or escrow mechanism that automates pre-sale refunds if the 200-box threshold isn't met. Critical — see §13 risk #4.
  3. Settlement infrastructure: design and deploy the multi-sig / on-chain settlement contract that automates the waterfall.
  4. UX prototype: build the pull → auto-buyback flow with default-to-instant-sell behaviour. Highlight the pre-sale max-ROI (+1,032%) in marketing copy.
  5. Legal review: confirm regulatory classification, including the pre-sale + refund mechanism.
  6. Stress tests: re-run floor-shock and demand-shortfall scenarios at finalised pricing before live launch.
  7. Pilot: launch with full instrumentation, then re-calibrate model parameters from observed user behaviour.

Appendix A — Model assumptions

See simulation_assumptions.py for the full declarative list. Highlights:

  • Floor prices held constant within the campaign (except in §12 stress tests)
  • Demand deterministic at 1,000 pulls (no stochastic demand modeling yet)
  • User acceptance Bernoulli, independent across pulls
  • No gas fees / opex / CAC costs modeled
  • Inventory restock is instantaneous at floor (no slippage)
  • Single-campaign analysis only (no multi-week carryover)
  • Refund-guarantee mechanism modeled as a hard binary gate (run or don't), not as a continuous probabilistic process

D12. Structural assumptions NOT in the model

These are real risks that the simulator does not capture. They're flagged here so reviewers know the limits of the analysis.

  • Floor-price volatility within a campaign — prices held constant except in explicit stress tests (§12)
  • ETH/USD volatility — locked at snapshot rate ($2,106)
  • Demand stochasticity — pull count is deterministic; we don't model variance in actual user volume
  • Gas, opex, CAC, marketing spend — not subtracted from operator P&L
  • Bad-actor behavior — no Sybil resistance, no insider trading, no front-running
  • Open-market restock slippage — every restock executes at floor instantly
  • Multi-week carryover — campaigns treated as independent; no learning effects across weeks
  • User-acceptance correlation — assumed independent Bernoulli across pulls; no herding or learning
  • Acceptance-rate dependence on discount — model holds acceptance constant when discount changes (in practice a wider discount would push acceptance down marginally)
  • Pudgy floor as systemic risk — 27% of pool value sits in one collection; a structural Pudgy event isn't modeled beyond the §12 shock test
